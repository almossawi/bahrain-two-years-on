Bahrain: Two Years On
Rollover for options

Bahrain is a small island with a population of 1.2 million, 46% of whom are citizens. Unlike most of its neighbors, Bahrain's history is one of pearl diving. Assyrian inscriptions from 2000 B.C. mentioned the island's pearls, which were a gem so unique to it that they lured the likes of Jacques Cartier [1].

For over a century, Bahrain has been the scene of conflict at the approximate rate of once every decade [2]. The civil unrest has centered around such issues as civil liberties and political freedom, as evidenced in this BBC video from 1956 [3]. In 1921, while Bahrain was still a British protectorate, Major A. P. Trevor wrote to the government of India about the country's exploding civil situation [4]. Later accounts by Sir Charles Belgrave told of similar unrest [5].

The current conflict began on February 14, 2011 and has so far resulted in the deaths of 106 civilians, which includes eight expatriates, and three police officers [6][7][8][9]. Of those 106 civilians, 19 were teenagers or younger; 21 were female. Additionally, up to 20 miscarriages have been attributed to the crackdown. This visualization depicts the 109 documented deaths of individuals aged one day or older, as reported by human rights organizations, the independent commission of inquiry and the government, abstracted to take the form of a palm tree. Mouse-over for more details; click the axis labels to show or hide the bands. Unless noted otherwise, the causes of death are from [9], either verbatim or paraphrased.

Causes of death

The top cause of death is tear gas inhalation. 40% of all deaths are a result of direct inhalation of tear gas or complications arising from that. Several videos on YouTube, such as this one, appear to show police officers firing into homes. The second highest cause of death is birdshot shells, which can be devastatingly lethal when used against people [16]. 15% of deaths are attributed to injuries caused by such shells, while 11% are attributed to other forms of gunfire such as live ammunition, rubber bullets and tear gas canisters. 17% of deaths have been a result of physical abuse or torture while in custody.

Deaths over time

The highest number of deaths in a single day occurred on March 16, 2011 when Gulf troops crossed into Bahrain. A nationwide crackdown claimed six lives and left hundreds or more injured. Prior to that, the bloodiest day had been February 17, 2011, or Black Thursday, when Pearl Roundabout was raided in the early morning hours. Of the 10,000 protestors who were camped there [14], four died and 300 were injured.
